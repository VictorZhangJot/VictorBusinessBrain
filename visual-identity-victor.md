## Brand Personality
Jobs of Tomorrow speaks to hiring managers who don't have time to wade through hype — they want a consultant who reads the market and tells them the truth, plainly. The visual identity needs to signal competence and calm authority before a single word is read: precise, restrained, and trustworthy, with the credibility of a firm that handles regulated, high-stakes hires (healthcare licensing, foreign worker compliance) without ever feeling cold or corporate. The palette draws on deep navy — the color of institutional trust in finance, law, and healthcare administration — paired with a clear teal accent that signals forward movement, since JOT's other vertical (AI/Data Centre) is explicitly about staying ahead of where talent markets are going.

## Color Palette

**Primary: Deep Navy — #14304D**
Strategic reason: Navy is the color hiring managers already associate with institutional reliability — banks, hospitals, government bodies. For a recruiter handling regulated healthcare placements and foreign-worker compliance, this signals "we know how to handle this correctly" before a word of copy is read.

**Secondary: Clear Teal — #1C8C8C**
Strategic reason: Teal sits between trust (blue) and growth (green) — it signals forward motion without veering into the generic "tech startup" green or the aggressive energy of orange/red common in recruitment marketing. It directly supports the AI/Data Centre positioning: ahead of the curve, not chasing it.

**Accent: Warm Amber — #D9913A**
Strategic reason: Used sparingly for highlights, CTAs, and key data points — amber provides warmth against the navy/teal coolness, preventing the brand from feeling clinical. It signals attention without urgency-driven alarm (avoiding red, which reads as "problem" rather than "opportunity").

**Neutrals:**
- Background: #0B1320 (near-black navy, not pure black — keeps the dark theme on-brand rather than generic)
- Surface/Card: #14202E
- Surface 2: #1B2A3A
- Muted text: rgba(255,255,255,0.55)
Strategic reason: A navy-tinted dark neutral palette keeps every surface visually tied to the primary color family, so the brand feels cohesive even in plain data tables or long-form text sections.

**Color Distribution:**
- Primary (Navy): 50% → backgrounds, headers, structural elements
- Secondary (Teal): 30% → section headers, pillar cards, data highlights
- Accent (Amber): 10% → CTAs, key stats, badges
- Neutrals: 10% → body text, borders, secondary UI

**Color Do's and Don'ts**
Do:
1. Use teal for anything tied to forward-looking claims (market trends, AI screening, "what's coming next")
2. Reserve amber strictly for calls to action and standout figures — never as a background fill
3. Keep navy as the dominant tone in any document cover or section header

Don't:
1. Never use red — it reads as alarm/risk, which undercuts the calm, consultative tone
2. Never let teal and amber appear in equal proportion — teal should always dominate over amber
3. Never use bright, saturated blue — it tips into generic "corporate tech" rather than the institutional navy this brand needs

## Typography

**Font 1 (Display / Headings): Libre Baskerville**
Weights to use: 700 (Bold)
Use cases: Document titles, section headers, pull quotes, cover pages
Strategic reason: A serif headline font signals the kind of institutional credibility hiring managers expect from firms handling licensed healthcare placements — closer to a law firm or hospital system than a startup. It reinforces "precise and considered," not "fast and flashy."

**Font 2 (Body / UI): Inter**
Weights to use: 400 (Regular), 500 (Medium), 600 (Semibold)
Use cases: Body copy, tables, labels, navigation, LinkedIn content
Strategic reason: Inter is built for clarity at small sizes and reads as clean and modern without personality overload — fitting Victor's own stated preference for content that is "precise, instead of beating around the bush."

**Font Pairing Logic:**
Libre Baskerville's serif weight gives section headers gravitas and a sense of institutional permanence, while Inter keeps every working sentence fast to read and unambiguous. The contrast mirrors the brand's actual positioning: serious and considered at the strategic level, clear and efficient at the operational level.

## Type Hierarchy
- H1: Libre Baskerville, 40px, Bold
- H2: Libre Baskerville, 28px, Bold
- H3: Inter, 18px, Semibold
- Body: Inter, 15px, Regular
- Label/Caption: Inter, 11px, Semibold, uppercase, letter-spaced

## Visual Direction

**Photography style:** Real workplace settings — hospital corridors, data centre floors, office meeting rooms — natural light, candid rather than posed, no stock-photo handshakes.
**Illustration style:** Not recommended for this brand — illustration tends to undercut the institutional credibility this brand needs; stick to photography, data visuals, and typography.
**Graphic style:** Structured and data-driven — clean grids, simple line icons, occasional bar/line charts to support market-trend claims.
**Layout style:** Airy but structured — generous whitespace, clear section dividers, never dense or cluttered, reflecting the "precise, not roundabout" voice.
**Icon style:** Outline icons, consistent stroke weight, no filled or 3D icon styles.
**LinkedIn content style:** Insight-led carousels and text posts that read like a market briefing, not a recruitment ad — data points and trend callouts in teal/amber against navy backgrounds.

## Brand Do's (10 items)
1. Lead every piece of content with a market insight before mentioning candidates or job openings.
2. Use specific numbers and timelines wherever possible (e.g. "three to five candidates within a couple of days") instead of vague claims.
3. Keep visual layouts uncluttered — one core idea per slide or section.
4. Use teal consistently for anything framed as "what's ahead" in the market.
5. Always state plainly that JOT does not charge candidates fees, wherever trust is being established.
6. Use real outcome stories (like the F&B placement) over generic testimonials.
7. Keep healthcare and AI/Data Centre content visually distinct through subtle photography choices, even within the same color system.
8. Use Libre Baskerville sparingly — only for true headlines, never body copy.
9. Maintain the navy-dominant palette across every touchpoint, from website to LinkedIn to PDF reports.
10. Let whitespace do the work — avoid filling every section with text or graphics.

## Brand Don'ts (10 items)
1. Never use the generic recruitment-industry cliché of stock photos showing handshakes or smiling office groups.
2. Never use bright primary blue or corporate-tech gradients — common in HR-tech and recruiter branding, and exactly what this brand needs to avoid blending into.
3. Never use red anywhere in the system.
4. Never default to sans-serif-only typography — the serif headline is part of what separates this from generic agency branding.
5. Never overcrowd a single page with multiple competing data visualizations.
6. Never use stock icons with inconsistent styles (mixing filled and outline).
7. Never let amber dominate a layout — it's a highlight color, not a base color.
8. Never use casual, hype-driven language in visual callouts ("ROCKSTAR HIRES," "ELITE TALENT").
9. Never apply the dark navy theme inconsistently — if one document is dark-themed, all should be.
10. Never use clip-art-style icons for healthcare or tech imagery — invest in custom or high-quality outline icon sets instead.
