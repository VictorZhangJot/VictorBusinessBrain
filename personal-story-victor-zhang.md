# PERSONAL STORY DOCUMENT
## Victor Zhang

---

## SECTION 1: ORIGIN & FOUNDATION

### The Moment That Changed Everything
One specific moment that changed how I think about work was realizing that you don't only buy things with money saved — you can buy them with money earned.

For a long time, I thought the way to afford the things I wanted was to slowly save up from my salary. But savings always seemed to get eaten up by insurance, daily expenses, holidays, and other commitments. I remember trying to save for my first Rolex, and it felt like it would take forever.

Then I earned my first big commission, and everything clicked. That one breakthrough moved me further ahead than years of saving would have. It made me realize that instead of only focusing on cutting back and saving more, I could focus on increasing my income by doing work I genuinely enjoyed and was good at.

That moment changed my mindset: there is a limit to how much you can save, but there is no fixed limit to how much you can earn.

### The Person Who Shaped Them
My father has been one of the biggest influences in the way I approach problems.

Sometimes, when I am stuck on an issue, I find myself thinking about how he would have approached the situation and what kind of solution he would have come up with. He taught me to break problems down into smaller parts so they become easier to understand and manage.

At the same time, he also taught me not to lose sight of the bigger picture. It is important to consider not only how to solve the immediate issue, but also the wider impact of the solution.

Even though he is no longer around, the way he taught me to think continues to guide me in both my work and life.

### The Value Running Their Decisions
From a young age, I was taught to focus on doing the right thing.

In my work, I make business decisions every day, whether they involve pricing discounts, business development, sales forecasting, or other areas. Regardless of the function I am handling, I have always made it a habit to internally review and challenge my own decisions before presenting them.

I ask myself whether the decision is fair, logical, aligned with the bigger picture, and ultimately the right thing to do. This mindset has helped me make more thoughtful decisions and take ownership of the outcomes.

---

## SECTION 2: CHARACTER UNDER PRESSURE

### The Hardest Chapter
The hardest moment in my life was losing my dad when I was 13.

From a young age, he was a stern and disciplined figure in my life. He taught me how to think critically, form my own train of thought, and be resourceful enough to find answers on my own. More than anything, he taught me to be independent.

When he passed, it felt like a lesson that was cut short. But the values and skills he instilled in me stayed with me. Since then, I have continued to carry that sense of independence, resourcefulness, and discipline into everything I do.

These qualities have shaped the way I work and have allowed me to become a strong contributor in my career.

### The Risk That Defined Them
I started my career in telesales and customer service — roles that gave me a foundation in communication and client handling, but did not require deep technical or specialized skills.

Over time, I began to realize that while these roles were valuable starting points, they might not equip me with the skills needed to advance far in my career. That was when I decided to take a risk and step out of my comfort zone by applying for product manager roles in IT companies.

The interviews were challenging. Even though I prepared extensively by reading up on the job scope, company background, and industry, I still faced difficulties because I did not have prior experience in product management. However, I persevered. After interviewing with several companies, I eventually managed to land the role.

I later asked the hiring manager why he chose to hire me despite my lack of experience. He told me it was my attitude that convinced him to give me a chance.

That opportunity became a turning point in my career. From there, I progressed steadily within the company and eventually moved on to higher roles in other organizations. Looking back, I believe my current achievements were only possible because I was willing to take a risk, leave my comfort zone, and challenge myself to grow.

### The Mistake That Built Them
I would not say that I have faced many major setbacks, but one mistake I can identify is allowing self-doubt to hold me back.

For a long time, I believed that starting a business was extremely risky and required a large amount of capital. That mindset became one of the biggest reasons I hesitated to step into entrepreneurship earlier.

Looking back, I sometimes wonder what could have happened if I had started my own business five years ago. Perhaps I would have gained more experience, built stronger networks, and learned valuable lessons much earlier.

This realization has taught me that while risks are real, overthinking and self-doubt can sometimes be even more limiting. Moving forward, I want to be more willing to take calculated risks, start small where necessary, and not let fear delay my growth.

### Standing Their Ground
When I joined the company as a Product Manager, I discovered on my first day that the business was operating at a significant loss of slightly over USD 1 million. This was not revealed during the interview process, but instead of being discouraged, I saw it as a challenge and took it upon myself to help turn the business around.

Within the first few months, I identified a major issue: there were no proper pricing controls in place. Competitive pricing information was inaccurate, and while the company was winning deals, many of them were likely quoted much lower than necessary. This resulted in thin and unsustainable margins.

To address this, I proposed a set of countermeasures to management. These included implementing stronger pricing controls, improving the accuracy of competitor pricing information, and setting clear criteria for reviewing tender pricing before submission.

The changes were not easy to implement. The sales team was uncomfortable with the new controls because it meant they had to spend more effort persuading clients instead of relying heavily on discounts. There was strong resistance at the beginning, and pushing the plan forward strained my relationship with the sales team.

Despite the resistance, I held my ground because I believed the changes were necessary for the long-term health of the business. After three months, the sales team began to see small wins. They were giving fewer discounts, securing higher selling prices, and improving margins without losing every opportunity.

Over time, the results became clear. After one year, the business managed to break even, with no losses reported for that year. By the end of the second year, the company had become profitable, and employees were able to receive their variable bonuses.

Although it took two years to fully see the benefits of the changes, the experience taught me the importance of making difficult decisions, staying firm when the direction is right, and building trust through results. While the relationship with the sales team was strained at first, we eventually worked together more closely, and I was able to win their trust by proving that the changes were beneficial for both the team and the company.

## SECTION 3: THE WORK & THE WHY

### When They Knew
I felt that this work was meant for me when we started winning major deals while still maintaining healthy margins.

That was when I realized it was never just about offering the lowest price. What mattered most was the value we were able to present to customers. I enjoyed going on joint visits with the sales team because it gave me the opportunity to hear directly from customers, understand their requirements, and listen to their feedback.

From there, I was able to position our product strengths more effectively and translate them into clear benefits for the customer. It gave me a strong sense of achievement whenever I was able to convince the customer, address their concerns, and help close the deal.

Those moments made me realize that I enjoyed not just managing the product, but also connecting business strategy, customer needs, and sales execution together.

### What They Do Differently
I may not always be the smartest person in the team, but I make it a point to put in the highest level of effort to ensure that my tasks are completed accurately and with minimal mistakes.

I take accountability for the work I submit and uphold integrity in everything I do. To me, completing a task is not just about getting it done, but making sure that it is properly thought through, well-structured, and reliable.

Because of this, my work is often able to go through management review smoothly. I make the effort to present data and recommendations from management's point of view, anticipating the questions they may have and ensuring that the information is clear, relevant, and useful for decision-making.

I am personally invested in the quality of the work I submit. Rather than seeing it as just another task completed, I see it as a reflection of my standards, ownership, and commitment to delivering work that others can trust.

### The Reframe That Changes Everything
The sales team often sees a quotation as completed once the pricing has been finalized. However, I have always emphasized to them that a strong quotation is not just about the final price, but about the value we are proposing to the customer.

Before submitting a quotation, I make the effort to understand the customer's pain points and identify the right angles to position our product's strengths. This allows us to present our solution in a way that directly addresses the customer's needs, rather than simply competing on price.

I also take the time to share with the sales team how I conduct competitor analysis and pricing simulations. This helps them understand the rationale behind our pricing and ensures that we are aligned when customers raise questions or challenge our proposal.

My goal is for the sales team to see the full picture behind every pricing submission. It is not just about quoting a number, but about understanding the customer, positioning the value correctly, and being confident in defending our proposal.

### The Work That Made Them Proud
There was a case where we were trying to engage system integrators who were not actively focused on selling our products. The account manager in charge was initially negative about the initiative, as he felt that the partner would not be interested in promoting our products.

Instead of accepting that assumption, I browsed through the partner's website and noticed that they had banners of other products displayed on their homepage. These banners had high visibility and could be a good opportunity for us to gain exposure.

I suggested to the account manager that we ask the partner whether we could secure a slot on their homepage. To make it easier for them, I proposed that we prepare the banner design ourselves, so the partner only needed to upload it onto their website. Although the account manager was hesitant, he eventually reached out to the partner. To his surprise, the partner agreed to the initiative immediately.

After the call, the account manager told me that he had not expected it to be so straightforward and realized that he should not have been negative from the start.

I then explained that once the homepage banner was completed, we could take the next step by offering product images and listing information for the partner's online shop. This was the value we wanted to show system integrators — that we would maintain constant communication with them, support their marketing efforts, and make it easy for them to list and promote our products.

As a result of this exercise, we eventually received a sizeable sales lead from the partner through the homepage banner initiative. More importantly, the account manager became more convinced of the importance of showing value to partners, instead of only judging them by the sales orders they were currently contributing.

This experience reinforced my belief that business development is not just about asking partners to sell more. It is about creating simple, practical ways for them to engage with us, making it easy for them to promote our products, and building trust through consistent value.

## SECTION 4: WORLDVIEW & DIRECTION

### What Everyone Gets Wrong
One of the challenges I face at work is dealing with careless mistakes from the sales team. While I understand that mistakes can happen, I find it difficult when work is done without sufficient thought, ownership, or effort.

At times, there is a tendency for the team to rely heavily on their managers or on me to check through their work, instead of taking full responsibility for the accuracy and quality of their own submissions. This can be frustrating because it creates unnecessary dependency and slows down the overall process.

This is quite different from the way I approach work. I value independence, structure, and accountability. I believe that everyone should take ownership of their own responsibilities, put in the necessary effort, and ensure that their work is properly reviewed before passing it on to others.

Because of this, I have learned to be more patient while still setting clear expectations. Rather than simply correcting mistakes for them, I try to guide the team toward being more careful, structured, and accountable in their work.

### How Success Changed Shape
In the past, I measured success mainly by personal results — the number of deals won, the revenue generated, and how much I had progressed within the company. These were the key indicators that defined whether I was doing well.

Over time, however, my definition of success has evolved. Today, I see success not only in my own achievements, but in how well I am able to develop the sales team's sales strategies, business acumen, and overall way of thinking.

I feel a strong sense of satisfaction when the sales team is able to think on the same wavelength, understand the bigger picture, and present information in a structured and strategic way — similar to how I would have approached it myself.

As I progressed in my career, I came to understand that meaningful change cannot be driven by one person alone. For change to be sustainable, the whole team needs to be aligned, capable, and moving in the same direction.

### Where This Is All Going
In my current role as a Product Manager, I can see that the market is shrinking due to declining demand. This appears to be part of a wider global trend, and realistically, it is something that may be difficult to reverse.

Because of this, I have started thinking more seriously about my long-term career direction and how I can build something of my own. I am planning to set up a recruitment agency, with a focus on the healthcare and AI/data centre sectors.

I chose these two areas because they are expected to see strong growth and manpower demand in Singapore. Healthcare will continue to require talent due to an ageing population and rising service needs, while AI and data centres are becoming increasingly important as businesses invest more in digital infrastructure and technology.

To me, this is not just about moving away from a shrinking market. It is about positioning myself in sectors with stronger future demand, while building a business where I can apply my experience in sales, business development, market analysis, and relationship management.

### What People Actually Get
Clients will be able to see the sincerity and thoughtfulness I bring to the work. I want them to see me not just as another recruiter, but as a problem solver who takes the time to understand their needs and provide meaningful solutions.

With my experience in the corporate space, I will also be able to advise clients on market trends that I foresee and add value beyond recruitment alone. By sharing the market insights I have gathered through my own work experience, I hope to position myself as a trusted partner who can support their hiring decisions with both practical advice and industry perspective.
